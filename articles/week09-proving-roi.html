How to Prove AI ROI to Your Board, Without the Buzzwords

Remco Vroom · May 3, 2026 · 8 min read

Only 14% of CFOs say they can clearly demonstrate the business impact of their AI investments. But among the 14% who are tracking it properly? 77% report a return of 2x or more. The problem isn't that AI doesn't work. The problem is that most organizations aren't measuring it.

Now for this week's deep dive...

The Silence That's Costing You

Here's what's happening in boardrooms everywhere right now:

Someone presents an "AI roadmap." There are slides. There's a timeline. There's enthusiasm. There may even be a live demo.

And then someone asks: "What's the return?"

Silence. Or worse, vague statements about "long-term transformation" and "building capabilities for the future."

That silence is costing your organization trust, budget, and competitive ground.

The answer isn't to stop investing in AI. The answer is to start measuring it properly.

The Context

A 2024 McKinsey study found that 35% of CEOs admit their AI projects create an "illusion of progress", activity that looks like transformation but isn't producing measurable outcomes. The industry even has a name for it: AI washing.

It's not fraud. It's usually just a measurement gap.

The organizations pulling ahead are doing something remarkably simple: they defined success before they started.

Three metrics. That's all it takes to begin.

1. Time saved. How many hours per week does this AI capability return to your team? At what fully-loaded cost per hour? Multiply across the year.

2. Quality improved. Is the output better? Are error rates down? Are customer satisfaction scores moving? Pick one measurable quality signal and track it.

3. Cost reduced. What were you spending before, on tools, on people, on rework, on third parties? What are you spending now?

That's it. Not a framework. Not a six-month consulting engagement. Three lines on a spreadsheet, tracked every 30 days, with a 90-day target agreed up front.

The Analogy

Imagine you hire a marketing agency. They run campaigns for three months. At the quarterly review, you ask: "Did this work?"

They say: "Oh, the team really learned a lot. We tested some new creative directions. The brand awareness is building. It's early days."

You'd fire them.

But in AI, we accept exactly this kind of non-answer, because "it's new" and "it's complex" and "these things take time."

They don't.

Not when you set 90-day targets. Not when you define your three metrics on day one. Not when you treat AI investment like any other capital allocation decision.

A marketing campaign without conversion tracking isn't a campaign. It's an experiment with no hypothesis.

AI without defined success metrics isn't a transformation. It's a budget line with a story attached.

These aren't projections. They aren't pilots. They're client results, with before-and-after numbers, measured the same way you'd measure any other business investment.

Our co-founder Wesley ter Haar put it directly: "An AI story that doesn't change economics isn't anything more than a story."

We're moving toward subscription-based delivery models, where you pay for continuous AI capability, not individual projects. Our target: subscription pricing representing 25% of our revenue by end of 2026. Why? Because it aligns our incentives with your results.

"If your team says 'AI is going great' but can't show you a number, it's not going great. It's going nowhere."

Your One Action This Week

Before your next AI budget conversation, whether you're approving spend, reviewing a vendor, or planning next quarter, do one thing:

Define exactly three metrics you will track. Set a 90-day target for each. Write them down.

Not "we expect efficiency gains." Not "we anticipate improved output quality."

Specific. Measurable. Time-bound.

Then hold the work to those numbers. Not in six months. In 90 days.

That's how 14% becomes your number, and 2x becomes your floor.
