Your Employees Are Already Using AI. Most of Them Aren't Telling You.

Remco Vroom · Mar 22, 2026 · 7 min read

Right now, while you're reading this, someone on your team is using an AI tool that isn't on your approved software list. They're not doing it to cause problems. They're doing it because it works.

Welcome back to SundAI Motivation.

This week we're talking about what I call the Shadow AI problem. It's the most under-discussed operational risk in business right now, and it's one of those situations where the risk is real, the solution exists, but most organizations are stuck because they haven't been willing to name what's actually happening.

Let me name it.

Your employees are using AI whether you've authorized it or not. Most of them know that. Many of them are quietly hoping you won't ask. And the way you respond to that reality, with restriction, with permission, or with something more thoughtful, will define a lot about how AI actually develops inside your organization.

The Deep Dive: The Shadow AI Problem (and Why the Answer Isn't "No")

The Simple Version

Your employees are using AI whether you've sanctioned it or not. The organizations that handle this well aren't the ones that banned it, they're the ones that moved faster to provide better, safer tools before their teams went looking for their own. Permission and governance travel together.

The Context

Let me walk you through the numbers, because they paint a picture that I think most leaders haven't fully seen yet.

57% of employees hide their AI use from their employer.

More than half. Think about what that means in practice. If you stood up in an all-hands meeting tomorrow and asked "who is using AI tools in their work?", statistically, nearly half your audience would stay silent or actively misrepresent what they're doing.

Why are they hiding it? The research is clear on this, and the answer matters: it's not because they're doing something malicious. It's because they've absorbed signals, through silence on the subject, through lack of clear policy, through a general culture of caution, that AI use might be viewed negatively, questioned by management, or used as evidence that they're not doing "real" work. So they use it anyway, quietly, and don't mention it.

Now, 87% of employees use AI tools at least weekly. 51% use them daily.

This is not a future state I'm describing. This is happening now, in your organization, today. AI is already embedded in how your team works. The question has moved past "are they using it?" to a much more urgent question: "are they using it safely?"

Here's where the risk materializes.

38% of employees report sharing sensitive company information with AI tools that haven't been approved or vetted. Customer data. Financial projections. Personnel information. Strategic plans. Being pasted into a consumer AI tool, processed by a third-party model, potentially stored on servers with no data sovereignty agreements, no enterprise privacy controls, no audit trail.

Most people doing this aren't thinking about the risk. They're thinking about the deadline they have in two hours and the fact that Claude just made their job significantly easier.

The IBM Cost of a Data Breach research quantifies what this exposure costs when it goes wrong: shadow AI adds an average of $670,000 to the cost of a data breach. Not because AI caused the breach, but because unsanctioned tools create data exposure that your security team doesn't know to monitor, can't see in their logs, and therefore can't respond to quickly.

And here's the gap that I find most revealing, the one that tells you where the real work needs to happen.

73% of executives believe AI use in their company is "well-regulated." When you ask employees the same question, only 47% agree. That's a 26-point gap between what leadership thinks is happening and what the people doing the actual work experience.

That 26-point gap is not primarily a compliance problem. It's a communication problem. Leadership has sent signals, perhaps issued a policy, perhaps had a few conversations, and concluded that the situation is managed. Employees are living in a different reality, where the policy exists but the tools, training, and genuine permission structure don't.

You cannot govern a gap you haven't acknowledged.

The Analogy

This has happened before, and not that long ago.

About 15 years ago, the same dynamic played out with cloud storage and collaboration tools. IT departments issued policies: "Don't use Dropbox. Don't use Google Docs. Use the approved file server." Those policies were issued with genuine intent, the approved systems were secured, audited, and compliant.

The problem was that the approved systems were also slow, clunky, hard to share across organizations, and completely unable to compete with the experience of using Dropbox or Google Docs. So employees smiled, nodded, and used Dropbox and Google Docs anyway. On their personal accounts. With no enterprise controls. Files sitting in consumer cloud storage, owned and operated by employees' personal Gmail addresses.

Companies that tried to solve this by doubling down on the ban, monitoring, restricting, disciplining, failed. The tools kept spreading underground. The risk got worse, not better, because employees got better at hiding their usage.

Companies that solved it did something different. They moved fast to adopt enterprise versions of the tools people were already using. They added proper access controls, data governance, and audit capabilities on top of the functionality their teams wanted. They turned the shadow into official infrastructure.

The answer to shadow IT was never "no." It was: "Yes, but here's the safe version. Here's the enterprise agreement. Here's the training. Here's what you can and can't put in it."

The answer to shadow AI is structurally identical.

Case Study

When we built our AI platform, governance wasn't added at the end as a compliance checkbox. It was designed into the architecture from the beginning, because we knew that at the scale we operate, with the types of client data we handle, governance had to be a feature, not an afterthought.

Data sovereignty is built in: client data is processed under the terms it's supposed to be processed under, in the jurisdictions where it's supposed to stay. There are no ambiguities about where the data goes or who can access it.

Human-in-the-Loop Governance is embedded in every workflow that involves sensitive decisions or significant client-facing output. The AI generates, accelerates, and proposes. The human reviews, approves, and is accountable for what ships. The speed advantage of AI is preserved; the judgment risk is managed.

But here's what I want to emphasize most: tooling is only half the equation. The other half is culture, and culture requires sustained investment, not a one-time announcement.

We run a program called the School of AI, an ongoing internal learning initiative that covers not just the mechanics of using AI tools, but the ethics, the judgment, and the responsibility that comes with using them well. Every week, we run "15 Minutes of Now" sessions across teams, short, practical, peer-led briefings on what's changed, what it means, and how we're applying it this week.

Across more than 6,500 employees in 33 countries, this has created something genuinely difficult to build: a culture where AI use is open, normalized, and talked about rather than hidden. Where people share what they're doing. Where mistakes in AI use are learning opportunities, not fireable offenses.

The outcome? 92% of our staff report that AI tools are genuinely useful to their work. That's not a survey result manufactured to look good in a pitch deck. That's the outcome of an environment where people can use AI openly, get trained on it continuously, and see the results in their own day-to-day work.

You cannot get to 92% usefulness if 57% of your people are hiding their usage. The numbers are incompatible.

"Your employees aren't hiding AI from you because they're being sneaky. They're hiding it because you haven't given them permission to use it openly."

Your One Action This Week

This week, have one conversation you've been avoiding.

Ask your team, your direct reports, your department heads, whoever reports to you, one simple question:

"What AI tools are you actually using in your work right now?"

No judgment. No policy discussion. No "well, is that on the approved list?" Just listen. Take notes. Be genuinely curious.

I promise you will learn more from that conversation about where your organization actually is with AI than you will from any strategy presentation, any vendor briefing, or any industry report.

And when people see that you're asking with genuine curiosity rather than looking for compliance violations, the honest answers come. That honesty is the raw material for everything that follows, the governance, the training, the tooling decisions, the culture.

You can't govern what you haven't acknowledged. You can't build guardrails for a road you're pretending doesn't exist.

Start with the question.
