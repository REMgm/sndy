The Biggest Threat to Your AI Strategy Isn't Technology. It's Your Own Team.

Remco Vroom · Apr 12, 2026 · 7 min read

I want to share a number that stopped me when I first saw it. 41%. That's the share of Millennial and Gen Z employees who admit, in anonymous surveys, to actively sabotaging AI initiatives at their companies.

Not quietly opting out. Not passively disengaging while appearing cooperative. Not failing to adopt tools they were trained on.

Actively sabotaging.

Intentionally slowing adoption, working around new systems, undermining efforts to change workflows, seeding doubt in colleagues.

If your first reaction is frustration, if you're thinking "why would people do this?", I want to ask you to hold that reaction for a moment. Because when you understand exactly why it's happening, the path forward becomes clear. And surprisingly, the solution is not complicated.

It's just harder than buying software.

What the Data Actually Tells Us

Let's be direct about the scale of what's happening inside organizations right now.

52% of employees fear AI-driven job displacement. That number has nearly doubled in the past year. Fear that doubles in twelve months is not a passing anxiety.

71% of Americans believe AI will cause permanent job losses. Not a temporary disruption. Not a skills transition. Permanent elimination of roles. Whether or not you believe that framing is accurate, it is what your team believes right now.

42% of C-suite executives say AI is "tearing their company apart." That's a remarkable number, almost half of senior leaders reporting internal fracture as a direct result of AI implementation. 68% say they're experiencing significant tension between departments.

And through all of this: only 23% of employees say they feel genuinely engaged at work.

The sabotage isn't malicious. It isn't irrational. It is a predictable, documented human response to being left out of a change that directly affects your livelihood.

Fear wearing a different coat.

The Analogy That Makes This Obvious

Imagine you're an employee, any level, any function, and your company announces an office relocation.

New city. New building. Effective in 90 days.

No explanation of why the move is happening. No clarity on whether your role still exists in the new structure. No information on what the new office setup looks like, who you'll be sitting near, or how the team will be organized.

Just: "We're moving. This is exciting for the company. Details to follow."

How would you respond?

Some people would update their CV and start networking. Some would mentally check out. Some, the ones who feel most threatened and most powerless, would start making noise. They'd push back in meetings. They'd quietly work against the thing they can't control, because working against it is the only power they feel they have.

That is what is happening inside organizations right now with AI.

This is not a resistance problem. It is a communication and trust problem. And those are solvable.

Case Study

This is one of the things I'm most proud of . It came from a strategic belief: if you want your organization to operate at AI speed externally, you have to be doing the internal work.

WesleyBot. We built an AI recruiter, modeled on our co-founder Wesley ter Haar's voice, values, and approach to evaluating people. Not a generic chatbot. An AI that carries the texture of our company's human leadership, capable of engaging candidates with nuanced, context-aware conversation. It has engaged more than 600 candidates.

School of AI. We didn't run a lunch-and-learn. We built a curriculum. Ongoing, structured, practical AI education for our entire global workforce.

"15 Minutes of Now." Regular, short-form sessions where our teams share what they're actually using AI for, right now, in their actual work. No theory. No vendor-sponsored case studies. Real colleagues, real tasks, real results.

AI Training Taskforce. A dedicated group responsible for ensuring no part of the organization gets left behind.

The result: 92% of our staff rate our AI training as useful. Not "interesting." Not "informative." Useful, meaning it changed how they work. We also hold our B Corp certification, which means we're externally accountable for how we treat our people.

"Your employees aren't resisting AI. They're resisting being uninformed, unsupported, and afraid."

Your One Action This Week

This week, I want you to have one honest conversation.

Not a town hall. Not a polished all-hands. Not a memo about the company's exciting AI journey.

One real conversation, with your team, or a small group, or even one person, where you ask a single question and then stop talking:

"What worries you about AI?"

Then listen. Fully. Without defending your strategy. Without explaining why their fears are overblown. Without pivoting to the opportunity.

Just listen.

What you hear will tell you more about the real state of your AI transformation than any consultant's readout, any technology audit, or any adoption dashboard.

That is not a soft move. That is the most strategically intelligent investment you will make this month.
