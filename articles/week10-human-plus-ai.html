AI Won't Replace Leaders. But Leaders Who Use AI Will Replace Those Who Don't.

Remco Vroom · May 10, 2026 · 7 min read

This week I want to address the fear that no one in the C-suite is saying out loud, but almost everyone is feeling. "Is AI coming for my job?" The honest answer: not in the way you think.

But there's a version of this story that should genuinely concern you. And it's not the one the headlines are writing.

Now for this week's deep dive...

The Simple Version

The fear is: AI gets smarter, jobs get automated, leaders become irrelevant.

The reality is almost exactly the opposite.

The leaders who will struggle aren't the ones whose tasks get automated. They're the ones who refuse to engage with the tools doing the automating.

This isn't a prediction about the future. It's already happening.

The Context

GitHub ran a study on developers using Copilot, their AI coding assistant. The results:

90% of developers said they felt more fulfilled in their work.

74% said they could focus more on creative and strategic tasks.

73% said they stayed in a state of flow, deep, productive work, for longer.

Think about that. The people most directly exposed to AI automation reported that their work became more meaningful, not less.

Why? Because the boring parts, the repetitive, mechanical, low-judgment tasks, got handed off. What remained was the work that requires a human: judgment, empathy, creativity, communication, and strategic thinking.

These are not skills AI is replacing. They are skills AI is surfacing, by clearing away the noise around them.

The Analogy

When spreadsheets arrived in the 1980s, accountants panicked.

The logic seemed airtight: if a machine can do the calculations instantly, who needs an accountant?

What actually happened? Accountants became strategic advisors.

The calculation work automated. The interpretation work, "what do these numbers mean for our business, what should we do next", couldn't. Demand for accountants with judgment, communication skills, and business acumen grew. The profession evolved.

The question isn't "will AI take my job?" The question is "am I developing the skills that AI can't replicate?"

The ATM Lesson

When ATMs arrived in the 1970s, bank tellers were supposed to disappear. And by pure logic, they should have.

But teller employment actually grew after ATMs arrived.

Here's why: ATMs made branches cheaper to operate, so banks opened more branches. And in those branches, tellers had more time for the thing they were actually good at, building relationships, understanding customer needs, solving complex financial situations.

Automation didn't eliminate the human role. It elevated it.

Case Study

At our organization, we've built our entire operating model around a simple principle: "People-in-the-model, not people out."

When we designed our AI platform, we didn't build it to replace our creative directors, strategists, and senior producers. We built it to remove the work they hate so they can do more of the work they're extraordinary at.

The PUMA AI-generated film: our human creative directors set the vision. They made every meaningful aesthetic and strategic decision. AI accelerated the execution, delivery was 2.8x faster than traditional production. The humans weren't bypassed. They were amplified.

We call this Human-in-the-Loop Governance. It's embedded in every our AI platform workflow. Not as a compliance requirement, but as a quality commitment, because human judgment at key decision points isn't a bottleneck. It's the point.

Our subscription pricing model doesn't just include AI agents. It includes senior talent and human judgment alongside those agents. We're not selling AI instead of people. We're selling the combination, because that's where the value lives.

The Skills That Matter More Now

If AI handles the mechanical and routine, here's what rises in importance for leaders:

Judgment under ambiguity. AI can give you 10 options. Choosing the right one for your specific context, with incomplete information, under time pressure, that's leadership.

Genuine empathy. Your teams are navigating real uncertainty right now. The ability to be present, to read what's not being said, to lead through change, AI cannot model this.

Creative direction. AI generates. Humans curate, elevate, and make it mean something. The skill of knowing what good looks like is becoming more valuable, not less.

Strategic communication. In a world where everyone has access to the same AI-generated analysis, the leaders who can synthesize it into a clear, compelling narrative will be the ones who move organizations.

Cross-functional thinking. AI optimizes within defined parameters. Seeing how sales, marketing, operations, finance, and culture interact is a deeply human capability.

None of these skills are being automated. All of them are being tested.

"AI doesn't make leaders less important. It makes leadership more important. Because when everyone has access to the same AI tools, the differentiator is the human behind them."

Your One Action This Week

Write down your job. Not your title, your actual job. The tasks, the decisions, the conversations.

Now mark everything that's mechanical, routine, or low-judgment. Those are the things AI will help with.

What's left is your real work. The work only you can do.

If AI handled all the routine parts of your job, what would you spend your time on?

Start practicing those things now. Not because your routine work is disappearing tomorrow, but because the leaders who are already investing in their irreplaceable skills are the ones building an advantage that compounds.

The window is open. Use it.
