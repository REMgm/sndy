Everyone Says They're "Doing AI." Almost Nobody Is Doing It Well.

Remco Vroom · Mar 15, 2026 · 8 min read

88% of business leaders say they're using AI. Only 1% say they're doing it well. That gap is the most important number in business right now.

Welcome back to SundAI Motivation.

This week I want to talk about something that almost nobody says out loud in boardrooms, but almost everyone is privately thinking: most AI initiatives are failing quietly.

Not dramatically, no press releases, no postmortems. They just... don't produce anything. The pilot runs. The demo goes well. Someone gets excited. And then, six months later, it disappears from the quarterly update deck and nobody mentions it again.

Understanding why that happens, and exactly how to avoid it, is the most valuable strategic advantage available to business leaders right now. Because the companies that crack this are pulling away fast.

The Deep Dive: Why AI Projects Fail (and What the Winning 1% Do Differently)

The Simple Version

The problem isn't the AI. The tools work. They work remarkably well. The problem is that most organizations treat AI like a feature to install rather than a capability to build, and there's an enormous, consequential difference between those two things.

The Context

Let me stack these numbers together for you, because individually each one is interesting, but together they tell a story that most business leaders haven't fully confronted.

88% of business leaders say their organization is actively using AI. Fine. But when you ask them how well it's going, only 1% describe themselves as mature or excellent at it.

Let that sit for a second. Nearly everyone is doing it. Almost nobody is good at it.

95% of AI pilots deliver zero measurable bottom-line impact. Not small impact. Not disappointing impact. Zero. They run, they get presented, they're called successful, and they produce nothing that shows up in the P&L.

42% of companies abandoned at least one AI initiative in 2025. That's up sharply from 17% the year before. Which creates this uncomfortable dual reality: more companies are starting AI projects and more companies are giving up on them, simultaneously. The churn rate is accelerating.

And the capstone number: only 6% of organizations can attribute more than 5% of their profits to AI. Six percent. After years of investment, after countless headlines, after "AI strategy" appeared in virtually every annual report, six percent of organizations have a line they can actually draw between AI and financial outcomes.

So how do we get here? How does an industry collectively spend billions of dollars, generate millions of words of strategy documentation, and end up with 6% of companies able to show a return?

The answer is simpler than most consulting firms would like you to believe.

Most organizations are optimizing for the appearance of progress rather than the architecture of results.

They run a pilot because a pilot is what you do. They call it a success because the demo worked. They announce it because it makes good content for LinkedIn. And they never build the organizational infrastructure, the integration, the training, the processes, the measurement, that turns a successful demo into a sustainable capability.

This is the gap between experimenting and operating.

Experimenting means: "Let's try this and see if it works." It's valuable for learning. It's terrible as an end state.

Operating means: "We've built this into how we work. We've defined what success looks like. We have someone accountable for it. We're measuring it weekly and improving it continuously."

Almost every company is experimenting. Almost none are operating. And the 1% who say they're doing AI well? They understood this distinction early and moved through the experimentation phase quickly, sometimes skipping it entirely, to build operating systems.

The Analogy

Let's talk about gym memberships.

Every January, gyms sign up millions of new members. Most of them stop coming by February. This is so predictable that gyms build their entire business model around it, they sell far more memberships than they could ever service simultaneously, because they know most people won't show up.

Is it a gym problem? Is the equipment broken? Is the fitness science wrong?

No. The gym works perfectly. The equipment is sound. The trainers know what they're doing. The system, if you actually use it consistently, will get you fit. The problem is everything surrounding the gym visit: no clear program, no accountability, no tracking, no connection between showing up occasionally and achieving a specific goal by a specific date. The tool works; the system around the tool is broken.

This is exactly where most companies are with AI.

ChatGPT works. Claude works. Google Gemini works. The underlying tools are genuinely capable. But you can't just hand someone a gym membership and expect them to get fit. And you can't just subscribe to an AI tool, let teams use it ad hoc, and expect transformation.

The people who actually get fit don't just "go to the gym." They define a goal, hire a trainer, follow a specific program, track their progress weekly, and build habits that sustain results past the first motivation spike.

That's what AI transformation actually requires. A program. Accountability. Measurement. Habits.

Monks Success Story

When Monks decided to go all-in on AI, we made a deliberate choice that I want to name explicitly, because it shapes everything that followed.

We didn't start with a pilot.

We started with a platform.

Monks.Flow wasn't designed to automate one task for one team in one market. It was designed to cover the entire marketing lifecycle, strategy, creative ideation, content production, deployment, performance optimization, and reporting, for clients at enterprise scale. The goal from the beginning was to change how the whole engine runs, not to add a feature to one cylinder.

That distinction matters enormously, and here's why.

When AI is embedded across a full workflow, outputs from one stage become inputs for the next. The system generates data. That data improves the models and the processes. Results compound over time. You're not extracting one-time efficiency gains from an isolated tool, you're building a machine that gets progressively better the more you run it.

Here's a concrete proof point. For Hatch, a growth-stage brand working with Monks, deploying Monks.Flow in combination with Google Gemini's capabilities delivered an 80% increase in click-through rate and a 31% decrease in cost per acquisition.

Those aren't pilot numbers run in a controlled environment. They're operating numbers from a system built to run at scale and improve continuously.

And the impact goes beyond individual client results. The reason Monks earned Adweek's first-ever AI Agency of the Year recognition isn't that we ran impressive demos. It's that we built a system that consistently produces results, because the system itself is designed for operations, not experimentation.

The companies that close the gap between experimenting and operating are separating themselves from the field right now. The gap is measured in months today. In 24 months, it will be measured in something much harder to close.

"The gap between companies experimenting with AI and companies executing with AI is the biggest competitive divide in business today."

Your One Action This Week

This week, do one honest audit, just for yourself, no slides required.

List every AI initiative, tool, subscription, or project your organization is currently running or has run in the past 12 months. It doesn't matter if the list is long or short.

For each item on the list, ask one question: "Is this running in production, with measurable results, that I could describe clearly to our CFO right now?"

Circle the ones where the honest answer is yes.

If you can't circle many, or any, that is not a reason for alarm. It's a diagnosis. Every organization that has successfully closed the experimentation-to-operation gap started with an honest assessment of where they actually were, not a polished version of where they wanted to be.

The audit itself isn't the work. It's the foundation for the work.

Be honest. That's the only way to start moving.
