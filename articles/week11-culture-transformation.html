How to Bring Your Whole Company Along, Without Breaking Your Culture

Remco Vroom · May 17, 2026 · 8 min read

This week I want to talk about the thing that kills more AI transformations than bad technology, bad timing, or bad vendors combined. Culture.

You can have the best tools. The clearest strategy. The strongest business case.

And still fail. Because you forgot to bring your people.

This Week in AI

Now for this week's deep dive...

The Simple Version

70% of digital transformations fail.

Not because the technology didn't work. Because the organization didn't change around it.

People felt threatened. Middle management resisted. Communication was poor. Training was an afterthought. Leadership talked about AI but didn't use it. The gap between what the executive team believed was happening and what employees actually experienced was enormous.

The good news: this is entirely preventable. And the companies doing it right are following five principles that any leadership team can apply.

The Context

  • A 26-point gap exists between how executives perceive their AI rollouts and how employees experience them. Leaders think it's going well. Employees feel unprepared and unsupported.
  • Only 7% of HR leaders are actively reskilling their workforces for AI. Seven percent.
  • 69% of employees prefer to learn new skills from peers, not from formal training programs or vendor demos.
  • Organizations that invest equally in cultural change and technical change report 5.3x higher success rates in their transformations.

5.3x. Not a marginal improvement. A category difference.

Five Principles That Work

1. Make it useful, not mandatory.

JPMorgan rolled out their LLM Suite to their entire workforce. No mandate. Just made it available, made it good, and communicated what it could do. Result: 250,000 people registered. 60% use it daily.

Ask yourself: Is our AI genuinely making people's work easier, or are we asking people to do extra work to use AI?

2. Use AI yourself. Visibly.

One in seven CEOs don't use AI tools in their own work. That sends a message, whether they intend to or not.

You don't have to be an expert. You have to be a practitioner.

3. Invest in people, not just platforms.

Most AI budgets are 95% technology and 5% people. That ratio needs to flip, or at least equalize.

The companies at 5.3x success aren't spending less on technology. They're spending more on helping their people learn, adapt, and find their role in an AI-augmented world.

4. Build a champion network.

Remember: 69% of people prefer learning from a colleague over a formal program. A champion network is how you scale peer learning across the whole organization.

Give them access, give them community, give them a platform to share what they're learning.

5. Communicate honestly.

The 26-point perception gap isn't a mystery. It's the result of leadership communicating what they hope is happening rather than what actually is.

Honest communication, including about uncertainty, about what you don't know yet, about real challenges, builds more trust than polished optimism.

The Analogy

Think about what happens when a company moves offices.

The decision gets made at the top. The logistics get planned. And then, if leadership is smart, they bring people along: town halls about the new space, input on design choices, clear communication about timing, support for the transition.

Or they don't. They announce it two weeks before moving day. People show up to a new building that wasn't designed with them in mind.

AI transformation is an office move at civilizational scale. The decisions are being made. The future is being built. The only question is whether your people feel part of it or subject to it.

Monks Success Story

In 2023, we made one of the most significant transformations in our company's history.

We went from MediaMonks, a holding company with multiple brands and entities, to Monks: a single, unified, AI-native brand.

We didn't just change the name. We reorganized around two practices and three go-to-market strategies. We restructured pricing, positioning, and delivery. We asked 6,500 people in 33 countries to see themselves differently.

We launched the School of AI. We ran weekly AI briefings. We built a Training Taskforce. Wesley ter Haar took on a dual operational role specifically to drive this agenda from the top.

We also created WesleyBot, an AI model trained on Wesley's thinking, so that strategic perspective could scale beyond one person's calendar.

The results were real: client retention rose from 85% to 92%. Our culture didn't break under the weight of transformation. It strengthened.

We maintained our B Corp certification throughout, because we believe AI transformation done right isn't just good business. It's good practice. We're committed to net-zero by 2040.

"Culture change isn't what happens after your AI transformation. Culture change IS your AI transformation."

Your One Action This Week

Before you approve your next significant AI investment, whether it's a platform, a vendor, a capability, ask one question in the room:

"Have we invested equally in helping our people through this change?"

Not equally in dollars, necessarily. But equally in seriousness, in attention, in planning.

If the answer is no, that's not a reason to stop the technology investment. It's a reason to start the people investment immediately, in parallel.

The organizations that will win the next three years aren't the ones with the best AI. They're the ones with the most prepared, most engaged, most confident people working with AI.

Build for that.
